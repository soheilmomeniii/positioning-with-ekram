## Second Existence

Every company exists twice:

1. Reality: the product, code, team, behavior, proof, and actual capability.
2. Story: what the market believes, repeats, funds, joins, or ignores.

Positioning authors the second existence. Most market action happens against the story, not the raw reality.

Diagnosis:

- What is true?
- What does the market currently think?
- What should the market think?
- What is the gap?

## Legible, Desirable, Inevitable

Marketing has three jobs:

1. Legible: the market understands what it is.
2. Desirable: the market wants it.
3. Inevitable: the market believes it is where things are going.

Order matters. Confused products cannot become emotionally powerful. Unwanted products cannot become inevitable.

## Four Questions

The four questions produce a position:

1. What is it?
   - Name the noun.
   - Avoid features, insider terms, and moods.
2. Why does it matter?
   - Translate mechanics into value.
   - Ask "so what?" until a human cares.
3. Why this, not the substitute?
   - Substitute beats competitor.
   - Include status quo, workaround, old habit, rival, or doing nothing.
4. Why now?
   - Name the timing force.
   - What changed? Why was last year too early? Why is waiting costly?

Positioning statement:

```text
[Name] is the [what] that [why it matters] for [who], unlike [substitute], because [why now].
```

## D x R x A

Test the position:

- Differentiated: does it stand apart? When the rival is a named company, read their actual copy before scoring this (see Market Check in SKILL.md). A claim a rival's homepage already makes is not differentiation.
- Relevant: does the market care?
- Authentic: can it prove the claim?

Treat weak as no. Multiplication means one weak term makes the whole position weak.

If Authentic is weak, stop and collect proof before writing copy: 3 to 5 concrete examples per measurable claim. The full proof gate, including the labeled provisional path, is in SKILL.md.

Most teams overestimate differentiation and underestimate relevance.

## Law Of Sacrifice

A position is made by subtraction. It sharpens by what it refuses.

Ask:

- What are we trying to own?
- What must we stop claiming?
- Who are we not for?
- Which revenue, audience, status game, feature, or use case are we willing to lose?

The sacrifice must hurt. If nothing is lost, the position is probably copyable.

Narrow is a wedge, not a ceiling. Win a small ownable space, then expand from strength.

## Jordan Vs. Thiel

After the position is sharp, choose the game.

Jordan:

- Compete inside an existing category.
- Win on a visible axis.
- Requires proof and defense.
- Example pattern: fastest, cheapest, safest, most liquid, best developer experience.

Thiel:

- Escape the old comparison.
- Create or own a new category or frame.
- Requires education and patience.
- Must avoid becoming a category of one.

The middle is mush. "Better and completely new" is usually unclear.

## Fork Test

Differentiation gets attention. Defensibility keeps it.

Ask what can be copied:

- code
- feature
- visual design
- pricing
- distribution tactic
- launch format
- category language

Then ask what is harder to fork:

- brand
- belief
- canonical market position
- founder symbol
- culture
- community
- trust
- narrative
- taste
- timing

Do not call a feature a moat. Assume features get copied.

## Category And Competition

Competition is a fight over frames, not only features.

Possible enemies:

- a company
- a category
- the status quo
- a behavior
- a belief
- an institution
- a technical architecture
- an old market assumption

For challengers, name the incumbent's weakness and make it historical: early, not evil. The enemy should feel obsolete, not merely bad.

For category creation, invite others in. A category with only one player is suspicious.

## End-To-End Sequence

Use this order:

1. Gather the seven facts.
2. Name reality, current story, desired story, and gap.
3. Diagnose legible, desirable, inevitable.
4. Answer the four questions.
5. Run D x R x A. Stop at the proof gate if Authentic is weak.
6. Force sacrifice.
7. Choose Jordan or Thiel.
8. Run the fork test.
9. Build brand, culture, narrative, founder, attention, and launch layers only as needed.
10. Produce the requested artifact.
