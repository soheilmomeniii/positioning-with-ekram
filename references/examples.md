# Examples

Use this reference for pattern matching, concrete copy, and avoiding generic outputs.

## Bad To Better

Bad:

```text
We are the AI-powered platform transforming blockchain infrastructure.
```

Better:

```text
Celestia is the first modular blockchain: launch your own chain without building the whole stack.
```

Why better:

- names the category
- avoids generic AI language
- creates a contrast with monolithic chains
- gives the market a phrase to repeat

Bad:

```text
The future of creator monetization.
```

Better:

```text
A pricing layer for creators who sell access, not attention.
```

Why better:

- names the customer
- names the substitute logic
- creates a sacrifice
- avoids empty future language

Bad:

```text
Best-in-class HR software that empowers your people strategy.
```

Better:

```text
Payroll that closes in one day, for companies running staff in five countries.
```

Why better:

- concrete outcome with a number
- names the exact buyer situation
- implies the substitute pain (multi-country payroll cycles)
- no inflated language, no borrowed template

## Case Patterns

### Celestia

Position:

```text
The first modular blockchain network.
```

Move:

- chose Thiel by escaping the monolithic L1 comparison
- owned "modular"
- carried an education burden
- made a technical architecture into a category

Lesson:

Do not describe a feature if a category can make the feature meaningful.

### Solana

Position pattern:

```text
The chain where speed makes new applications possible.
```

Move:

- chose Jordan inside L1s
- made speed the axis
- sacrificed decentralization-maximalist approval
- built a culture around performance and builders

Lesson:

If you choose Jordan, the proof must be visible and defensible.

### Stripe

Position pattern:

```text
Payments infrastructure for developers.
```

Move:

- narrowed to developers
- sacrificed generic buyer breadth
- earned brand through API quality and documentation

Lesson:

The emotional brand came from repeated product behavior, not declared vibes.

### Liquid Death

Position pattern:

```text
Murder your thirst. Canned water for people who hate wellness branding.
```

Move:

- took the most commoditized product on earth (water) and refused the category's codes
- chose one emotion (rebellion) and performed it everywhere: name, cans, copy, merch
- sacrificed the wellness-aesthetic buyer entirely
- enemy frame: plastic bottles and the pastel health-brand consensus

Lesson:

In a commodity category, the brand is the differentiation. Anyone can can water. Nobody can fork the tribe. This is the fork test passed on culture alone.

### ServiceTitan

Position pattern:

```text
The operating system for the trades.
```

Move:

- picked the market software people ignored: HVAC, plumbing, electrical contractors
- relevance first: solved scheduling, dispatch, and invoicing pain the buyer already felt daily
- wedge discipline: one trade deep, then adjacent trades from strength
- earned trust through vertical depth no horizontal tool could match

Lesson:

Boring markets reward specificity. "For the trades" did more work than any feature list. If the customer feels seen, the category can be unglamorous.

### Atomic Habits (book / creator)

Position pattern:

```text
Tiny changes, remarkable results. Systems, not goals.
```

Move:

- escaped the "self-help" shelf by owning a mechanism (habit systems), not a mood
- enemy frame: goals and motivation as the broken assumption
- coordination signal: "1% better every day," repeatable by readers who never opened the book
- the maxim spine did the marketing; every chapter compressed to a line that travels

Lesson:

A book is positioned by its repeatable line, not its table of contents. Creators and books follow the same law: own one mechanism, give believers the sentence.

### Segway

Failure:

- high product novelty
- low legibility
- unclear customer and substitute
- no believable why now

Lesson:

A brilliant thing can die if the second existence never forms.

### Clubhouse

Failure pattern:

- feature felt differentiated
- moat was weak
- competitors copied the mechanic
- narrative did not harden into durable category ownership

Lesson:

Feature novelty is not defensibility.

## Copy Patterns

### Homepage Hero

Use:

```text
[Name] is the [category] for [specific audience] who need [specific outcome] without [specific substitute pain].
```

Then:

```text
[Proof or mechanism]. [Why now or urgency].
```

Avoid:

```text
Build the future of [category] with seamless AI-powered infrastructure.
```

### Launch Line

Use:

```text
Today we are launching [thing]: [sharp position].
```

Then:

```text
The old way was [enemy/problem]. The new way is [future delta].
```

### Founder Narrative

Use:

```text
I kept seeing [pain/false assumption]. Everyone treated it like [old category]. The real problem was [insight]. So we built [thing] for [people], starting with [sacrifice].
```

### Category Thesis

Use:

```text
[Old category] was built for [old world]. But [new force] changed [constraint]. That creates room for [new category]: [definition].
```

### Book / Creator Line

Use:

```text
[Maxim the reader can repeat]. [Mechanism, not mood]. For [reader] who tried [substitute] and [why it failed them].
```

Avoid:

```text
The ultimate guide to transforming your [topic] journey.
```
